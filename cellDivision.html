Cell Divison

There are two main ways of cell division. These two types of cell division are mitosis and meiosis. When mitosis occurs, the one cells is just split into two, creating two clones of the first cell. When meiosis occurs, one cell is turned into four sex cells.

Mitosis

Definition: A type of cell division that results in two daughter cells each having the same number and kind of chromosomes as the parent nucleus, typical of ordinary tissue growth.

Stages of Mitosis

Prophase
Mitotic spindle (spindle fiber) forms
- Mitotic spindle is the a bipolar structure that segregates the chromosomes in mitosis.
Chromosomes condense in the middle of the cell
- A chromosome is a DNA molecule with the genetic code to give instructions to their specific cell.
Nucleolus disappears and the nuclear envelope is broken down
- The nuclear envelope is the double-layered membrane enclosin the nucleus of a eukaryotic cell.
Metaphase
Chromosomes line up at metaphase plate
- The metaphase plate is a section in the equatorial plane of the metphase spindle having the chromosomes oriented upon it.
Anaphase
The microtubules push the poles apart
- The microtubules pull chromosomes apart and towards the poles.
Telophase
- Spindle fiber disappears
- A nuclear membrane reforms around each set of chromosomes
- A nucleolus reappears in each new nucleus
- The chromosomes start to condense as the cell starts to split
Cytokinesis
Cytokinesis in animal cells:
- An actin ring around the middle of the cell pinches inward, creating an indentation called the cleavage furrow.
Cytokinesis in plant cells:
- The cell plate forms down the middle of the cell, creating a new wall that partitions it in two.

Cancer and Cell Regulation

Checkpoints

The cell cycle is regulated by checkpoints, which are factors the cell consideres when deciding whether or not to move forward through the cell cycle, and include both external cues and internal cues.

Cancer

Definition: A disease caused by an unctonrolled division of abnormal cells in a part of the body.

Cancer is caused by two main types of cell cycle regulator mutations.
Positive regulators, which normally promote cell growth, may become hyperactivated (oncogenic).
Negative regulators (tumor suppressors), which prevent the formation of tumors, may become inactivated.

Meiosis

Definition: A type of cell division that results in four dughter cells each with half the number of chromosomes of the parent cell, as in the production of gametes and plant spores.

Stages of Meiosis

Meiosis I
Prophase I
- The homologous chromosomes pair up and exchange fragments
Metaphase I
- The homologous pairs line up at the metaphase plate
Anaphase I
- The homologous pairs separate to opposite ends of the cell
- Sister chromatids stay together
Telophase I
- Each chromosome has two non-identical sister chromatids
Meiosis II
Meiosis I is repeated with both cells created in Meiosis I, creating four cells in all.
