Defining Life

Life can be defined in many ways, but there is a list of characteristics that scientifically defines life. The characteristics are:

It responds to the environment
- All living things detect change in their environment
- Ex. If you step close to a mouse, it will respond by running away.
It grows and develops
- All living things grow and develop
- Ex. A tadpole slowly grows into a frog
It produces offspring
- All living things are capable of reproduction
- Ex. A single cell splits into two daughter cells
It maintains homeostasis
- All living things are capable of maintaining a stable internal environment
- Ex. The human body shivers when it is cold to maintian a stable internal temperature
It has complex history
- All living things consists of large, complex molecules
- Ex. The human body has metabolism, which is the accumulated total of all the biochemical reactions occuring in a cell or organism
It consists of cells
- All living things consist of atleast one cell
- Animals and humans have different types of cells, but they still have cells

Principles of Biology

The Unifying Principles of Biology are what form the basis of biology. There are four principles of biology, which are:

Cell Theory
- The theory that all living things are made up of cells
Gene Theory
- The theory that the characteristics of living organisms are controlled by genes
Homestasis
- The idea in which a living thing maintains a stable internal environment
Evolution
- the change in the characteristics of living things over time

Interdependence of Living Things

All living things depend on other living things to survive. For example, all animals rely on plants to provide them oxygen. This is called interdependence. Interdependence comes in two forms:

Symbiosis
- A close relationship between organisms of different species in which at least one of the organisms benefits
- Ex. Birds eat parasites from a deer's fur
Competition
- A relatinship between living things that depend on the same resources
- Ex. Animals in a desert rely on water, but only the best get the little water that is in a desert

Molecular Biology

Molecular biology is the base of all biology for one main reason: all living things are made up of molecules

Carbon
Carbon is essential to life on earth because of its ability to form stable bonds with many elements.
Over 10 million carbon-based compounds
Four major types of carbon-based compounds: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids.
Carbohydrates
A carbohydrate is an organic compound such as sugar or starch, and is used to store energy.
Monosaccharides are simple sugars such as fructose or glucose.
Disaccharides are two monosaccharides bonded together
Polysaccharides are complex carbohydrate molecules that form when simple sugars bind together in a chain.
Complex carbohydrates have two main functions: storing energy and forming structures of living things
Proteins
A protein is an organic compound made up of small molecules called amino acids
There are 20 different amino acids commonly found in the proteins of living organims.
A polypeptide is a long chain of bonded amino acids. A protein consists of one or more polypeptide chains.
Proteins serve many different roles in living things. Some of these rolls are:
- Helping cells keep their structure
- Make up tissue cells
- Sending and recieving signals
- Some, called enzymes, help speed up chemical reactions in cells
- Some, called anitbodies, target substances like bacteria for destruction
- Carry messages
- Transport materials
Lipids
A lipid is an organic compound such as fat or oil.
Lipids are mainly used to store energy, and consist of repeating units called fatty acids.
There are two main fatty acids: saturated fatty acids and unsaturated fatty acids.
Saturated Fatty Acids
- Carbon atoms are bonded to as many hydrogen atoms as possible.
- Very straight, creating a solid at room temperature.
- Used by animals to store energy
Unsaturated Fatty Acids
- Carbon atoms are bonded to hydrogen atoms in groups, causing the chains to bend.
- Liquid at room temperature
- Used by plants to store energy
There are different types of lipids that also contain alcohol or phosphate groups. These lipids are:
- Triglycerids
- Phospholipids
- Steroids
Nucleic Acid
A nucleic acid is an organic compound, such as DNA or RNA, that is built of small units called nucleotides.
Many nucleotides binded together form a chain called a polynucleotide. RNA consists of just one polynucleotide chain. DNA consists of two polynucleotide chains.
Each nucleotide consists of three smaller molecules:
- Sugar
- Phosphate groups
- Nitrogen base
There are four different types of nitrogen bases, which are:
- Cytosine
- Adenine
- Guanine
- Thymine
Hydrogen bonds between complementary nitrogen bases, which are: Cytosine+Guanine and Adenine+Thymine.
The role of nucleic acid is to store and show the correct sequence of amino acids in a protein.
Biochemical Reactions
A chemical reaction is a process that changes some chemical substances into others.
Two parts of a chemical reaction:
- Reactant: the substance that starts a chemical reaction
- Product: the substance that forms as a result of a chemical reaction
In a chemical reaction, the quantity of an element doesn't change; there is the same amount of elements in the products as there was the reactants.
