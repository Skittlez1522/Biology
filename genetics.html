Predicting Heredity

Predicting heredity is mostly dependent on Mendel's Laws. These laws are the basis for how genetics and heridity work.

Mendelian Inheritance

Mendelian inheritance refers to the inheritance of traits controlled by a single gene with two alleles, one of which may be completely dominant to the other.

Genotype
A genotype is all of the alleles a individual inherits.
Homozygote: an organism with two alleles of the same type (BB)
Heterozygote: an organism with two different alleles (Bb)
Phenotype
A phenotype is the expression of an organism's genotype.
In a heterozygote, the dominant allele is expressed while the recessive allele isn't.

Non-Mendelian Inheritance

In Non-Mendelian inheritance, Mendel's Laws do not apply. Traits are not controlled by a single gene, there can be more than two alleles to a gene, and there are different types of dominance.

Types of Dominance
Codominance
- Occurs when both alleles are expressed equally in the phenotype of a heterozygote.
Incomplete Dominance
Occurs when the phenotype of the offspring is a mix between the phenotypes of both parents. This means a completely dominant allele does not occur.
Multiple Alleles
Polygenic Characteristics
- Characteristics that are controlled by more than one gene that can have more than two alleles.
- If the genes are located on the same chromosome, they are likely to be inherited together, even though it is possible they will be separated by crossing-over during meiosis.
- If genes are non-homologous chromosomes, they may be recombined in various ways because of independent assortment.
Environment vs. Phenotype
Phenotype is also controlled by other factors, such as the environment. Two examples of phenotypes that can be affected by the environment are:
- Height: poor nutrition can affect how tall humans grow
- Skin Color: the amount of exposure to the sun can increase the amount of pigment in skin and make it appear darker

Genetic Linkage

Linked genes are genes that are on the same chromosome. Alleles for the genes tend to segregate together during meiosis and are only sometimes separated by crossing-over. Crossing-over occurs when two homologous chromosomes exchange genetic material during meiosis I.

Sex-Linked Genes
Sex-linked genes are genes located on the sex chromosomes. Moste of them are on the X chromosome because the Y chromosome has relatively few genes.
Sex-linked genes are actually X linked genes, but are normally referred to as sex-linked genes.
Linkage Map
Linkage is assessed by determining how often crossing-over between two genes on the same chromosome occurs.
Non-Homologous Chromosomes
- Not linked
- Assort independently during meiosis, so they have a 50% chance of ending up in different gametes.
Homologous Chromosomes
- Linked
- Show up in different gametes less than 50% of the time, so they are assumed to be inherited together.
