Transport

There are two main ways molecules are transported inside the cell. The first is through passive transport and the second is through active transport.

Passive Transport

Passive transport is a type of transportation does not require energy. There are multiple examples of passive transport including:
- The difference in concentration between the inside and outside of cells, making the concentration even out inside and outside the cell.
- Osmisis: the movement of water molecules across the cell membrane.

Active Transport

Active transport is a type of transportation that does require energy. There are multiple types of active transport including:
- Exocytosis is the process in which a large amount of molecules are moved out of the cell by secreting them through an energy-dependent process.
- Endocytosis is the process in which a large amount of molecules are moved into the cell through an energy-dependent process.

Types of Osmosis

Osmosis is a process by which molecules of a solvent tend to pass through a semipermeable membrane from a less concentrated solution into a more concentrated one, thus equalizing the concentrations on each side of the membrane. There are three different states one side of osmosis can exist in. These are:

Hypertonic
- The hypertonic state of osmosis is when the side of the membrane in question has a higher concentration of molecules than the other side.
Hypotonic
- Contrary to the hypertonic state of the side of the membrane in question, the side is in a hypotonic state when it has a lower concentration of molecules than the other side.
Isotonic
- When one side of the membrane in question has the same concentration as the other, it is in a isotonic state, meaning both sides have the exact same concentration of molecules. The isotonic state of both sides of a membrane is the final product of osmosis.

Energy in Plants

The energy consumed by plants is created within the plant and not consumed. This is happens through photosynthesis which is a process in which plants mainly use sunlight, to create glucose and ATP, otherwise known as Adenosine Triphosphate.

Glucose & ATP

Photosynthesis is the process by which energy is created within a plant. The two main types of energy photosynthesis creates are glucose and ATP.

Glucose
- The chemical formula for glucose is C6H12H6. It is the "deliverable" form of energy produced by photosynthesis, yet it is also used in almost every other type of life in this world, making it a universal food.
ATP
- ATP is considered the "energy currency" of the cell because it is capable of releasing just the right amount of energy to fuel the processes within the cell.

Stages of Photosynthesis

Stage I - Light Reactions
- This stage uses water and changes light energy from the sun into chemical energy stored in ATP and NADPH, while releasing oxygen as a bidproduct.
Stage II - Calvin Cycle
- This stage combines carbon from carbon dioxide in the air and uses the chemical energy in ATP and NADPH to make glucose.

Chloroplast

The location in which photoynthesis occurs is the chloroplast. This happens in the internal membranes that stack on top of each other within the chloroplast.

Structure of Chloroplast
Grana
- Grana consists of thylakoid membranes, which are sac-like membranes that are used during the first stage of photosynthesis.
- Thylakoid membranes contain photosystems, which are groups of molecules that include chlorophyll, the pigment that makes plants green.
- The second phase of photosynthsis occurs outside of the thylakoid membranes in a space called the stroma.

Energy in Animals

Unlike plants, animals consume energy throught the process of eating and drinking. Even though the process of making or consuming energy is different, both plant and animal cells use ATP.

Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration is the process in which energy is released in glucose to make ATP. This seemingly simple, yet actually complex process is the basis for all living animal cells.

Stages of Cellular Respiration

Glycolysis
- During glycolysis, a glucose molecule is broken down to form two molecules of pyruvic acid, which are called pyruvate.
Krebs Cycle/Citric Acid Cycle
- During the Krebs cycle, the two molecules of pyruvate are then oxidized, creating two acetyl-CoA molecules and leaving two molecules of carbon dioxide.
Electron Transport Stage
-During the electron transport stage of cellular respiration, energy from NADH and FADH2 is transferred to ATP. THe energy is used to pump hydrogen ions across the inner membrane of the mitochondria, from the matrix into the intermembrane space.

Structure of a Mitochondrion

Inner membrane
- The inner membrane is the second membrane, only enclosed by the outer membrane.
- The third stage of cellular respiration takes place on the inner membrane.
Matrix
- The matrix is the space enclosed by the inner membrane. It consists of most of the objects within the mitochondira, including ribosomes, granules, and DNA.
- The third stage of cellular respiration takes place in the matrix.
